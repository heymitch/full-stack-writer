# Niche Positioning Frameworks

## The Core Law

**Specificity is the advantage.**

The more specific (niche) something is, the more likely it is to be seen as different.
The less specific (general) something is, the more likely it is to be seen as the same.

"I write about business" → invisible.
"I write about how solopreneurs can build a $100k/year business using a newsletter as the only marketing channel" → findable, memorable, subscribable.

More specific = more money. No exceptions.

---

## Framework 1: Your Newsletter Is About DOING, Not Learning

The #1 mistake in newsletter positioning: "I share my learnings about X."

Nobody wakes up excited to "learn." People want to accomplish something.

Ask: **what does your reader want to DO with the knowledge you're giving them?**

Reframe all positioning from "learning" to "doing":
- Not: "Learn how to write online" → Yes: "Start writing online and build an audience"
- Not: "Learn LinkedIn strategy" → Yes: "Grow your LinkedIn following to 10k in 90 days"
- Not: "Learn about financial freedom" → Yes: "Build a portfolio that replaces your salary"

Frame your newsletter around the outcome the reader is trying to reach.

## Framework 2: The 9 Ways to Niche Down

Take any broad topic and narrow it using one or more of these dimensions:

| Dimension | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Experience level** | Start (beginners) / Scale (intermediate) / Exit (advanced) |
| **Revenue level** | First $1 / $10k/month / $100k/year / $1M business |
| **Platform / tool** | On LinkedIn / With Notion / In Python / On Substack |
| **Location** | In Chicago / In Europe / In remote work environments |
| **Demographic** | For women / For college students / For people over 50 |
| **Life event / identity** | For new parents / For recently laid-off workers / For career changers |
| **Career path / title** | For Project Managers / For CMOs / For freelance designers |
| **Problem** | For entrepreneurs who failed before / For writers with imposter syndrome |
| **Desired outcome** | For developers who want to ship their first SaaS / For writers who want a book deal |

Pick the dimension that reflects where the deepest pain and highest motivation live.

## Framework 3: Top 10 Problems + Top 10 Outcomes

To find the right niche-down, do this exercise:

**Step 1:** List the Top 10 biggest problems your target reader faces.
**Step 2:** List the Top 10 most desirable outcomes they want to achieve.

Look at both lists and find:
- Problems you know how to solve
- Outcomes you know how to unlock
- Problems and outcomes that intersect with your genuine excitement

That intersection is your newsletter's positioning. Not the "most popular" problem—the one you're best positioned to solve AND most excited to write about.

If you're not excited about it, you won't be consistent. If you're not consistent, you won't succeed.

## Framework 4: The 1-Liner Formula

Your newsletter's 1-liner is the sentence that immediately communicates who you serve, what problem you solve, and what outcome you deliver.

**Formula:**
`[Newsletter Name]: [What you help readers DO], even if [common obstacle].`

**Or:**
`For [specific reader] who want to [specific outcome] without [specific obstacle].`

**Examples:**
- "For freelance writers who want to build an audience on LinkedIn without posting every day."
- "The newsletter that helps early-stage founders land their first 10 paying customers, even without a marketing budget."
- "Weekly frameworks for solopreneurs building a $100k/year business through writing."

Test your 1-liner: can a stranger understand exactly who it's for and what they'll get? If not, it's still too vague.

---

## Framework 5: Naming Your Newsletter

A great newsletter name does one or more of these:

1. **Names a new category** — "The Digital Writing Compass," "Category Pirates"
2. **States the outcome directly** — "Scale to $100k," "Start Writing Online"
3. **Claims a specific identity for the reader** — "The Creator Economy Insider"
4. **Creates a proprietary feeling** — makes it feel like a branded asset, not a blog

**Naming rules:**
- No generic names like "The [Industry] Newsletter" — forgettable
- No your-own-name newsletters unless you're already famous — the reader can't tell why it's for them
- Avoid abstract concepts that require explanation
- Test: does it immediately communicate something specific?

**Category Copywriting bonus:** Name a concept in your subject line to make emails feel like tangible assets.
- Generic: "3 tips for consistent writing"
- Named: "The Consistency Code: 3 daily habits that make writing feel effortless"

When you name something, you own it.

---

## Framework 6: Competitive Differentiation

Before finalizing positioning, find 3-5 newsletters in your space. For each:
- What's their 1-liner?
- Who are they primarily serving?
- What's the angle they're NOT covering?

Your positioning should make you the only obvious choice for a specific reader. If your positioning overlaps significantly with an existing newsletter, narrow further until there's clear daylight between you and them.
