# Newsletter Formats

## The Two Types That Work

There are only two newsletter types that attract and retain readers. Everything else—"I share my learnings along the way!"—loses subscribers.

---

## Type 1: Curation Newsletter

**Core promise:** Save the reader time. You do the research; they get the summary.

**Best for:**
- Fast-moving, newsy categories (stocks, politics, sports, pop culture, trending tech)
- Writers without deep domain expertise yet—curation is an easier starting point
- Giant, non-niche categories where aggregating many sources creates value

**Structure template:**

```
Hey {Reader}!

This week's roundup is all about {topic}:
- {Subtopic preview 1}
- {Subtopic preview 2}
- {Subtopic preview 3}

If you're {reader situation / goal}, here's what you need:

WEEKLY RESOURCE LIST:
1. {Resource + link} ({read time}) — {1-sentence why it matters}
2. {Resource + link} ({read time}) — {1-sentence why it matters}
3. {Resource + link} ({read time}) — {1-sentence why it matters}
4. {Resource + link} ({read time}) — {1-sentence why it matters}

FAVORITE TIP OF THE WEEK:
Here's my top tip about {topic} this week.

From {Source}: {What changed how you think about the problem}

Quick breakdown:
- Step 1: {Explain}
- Step 2: {Explain}
- Step 3: {Explain}

OTHER {INDUSTRY} NEWS:
- {News item}: According to {source}, {what happened}. This matters because {why}. I think it means {hypothesis}.
- {News item}: [same format]

That's it! Hit reply and tell me what you found most useful.

See you {day},
{Name}
```

**Monetization:** Primarily ads/sponsorships. Volume game.

---

## Type 2: Original Thinking Newsletter

**Core promise:** Accelerate the reader's expertise. Your frameworks, your perspective, your depth.

**Best for:**
- Slow-moving categories (writing, philosophy, strategy, history, psychology)
- Writers with genuine domain expertise and strong opinions
- Oddly specific niches where depth beats breadth

**Structure template:**

```
Hey {Reader}!

This week I want to talk about {topic}:
- {Main point 1}
- {Main point 2}
- {Main point 3}

The reason people misunderstand {topic} is {common misconception}. But once you understand {reframe}, you unlock {outcome for reader}.

Let's dive in.

{BIG IDEA}
In order to {accomplish goal}, avoid these common mistakes:
- {Mistake 1}: {Explain}
- {Mistake 2}: {Explain}
- {Mistake 3}: {Explain}

People make these mistakes because {underlying reason}. And as a result, {unfortunate outcome}.

So here's how to fix it:

{MAIN POINT 1}
The first step to {achieving goal} is {Step 1}.

{Why this matters}
{The myth or mistake that keeps people stuck here}
{What to do instead}
{A clear example}
{Takeaway for the reader}

{MAIN POINT 2}
[Same structure as above]

{MAIN POINT 3}
[Same structure as above]

That's it! Hit reply and tell me what resonated.

See you {day},
{Name}
```

**Monetization:** Can go paid. Quality game, not quantity.

---

## Combining Both Types

You can blend curation and original thinking—but know which one is your primary value. Readers subscribe for one or the other. Knowing which saves 10x the writing effort.

Stress test: if someone had to describe your newsletter to a friend in one sentence, would they say "it saves me time finding X" or "it makes me way smarter about X"?

That's your primary type.

---

## The Viral Loop Principle

Newsletter alone has no viral loop. Social platforms have distribution. Your newsletter has depth.

The relationship: social posts attract readers → newsletter converts them into subscribers → newsletter is the expanded, more valuable version of what they loved on social.

Never write something in your newsletter that has no connection to your social content. The newsletter is the director's cut. Social is the trailer.
