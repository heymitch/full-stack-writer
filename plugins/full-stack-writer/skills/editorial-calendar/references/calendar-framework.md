# 90-Day Editorial Calendar Framework

## Why 90 Days

90 days = 12-13 newsletter issues. That's enough to establish all your content pillars, test multiple formats, and build a clear sense of what resonates with your audience.

The goal isn't to plan every word in advance. It's to see how much ground you can cover—so you stop staring at a blank page every week.

---

## The 3-Step Calendar Generation Process

### Step 1: Core Topic Expansion

Apply the 10 Magical Ways to each of your content pillars:

For each pillar, generate angles using all 10 frames:
- Tips related to [topic]
- Stats about [topic]
- Steps for [topic]
- Lessons about [topic]
- Benefits of [topic]
- Reasons for [topic]
- Mistakes related to [topic]
- Examples involving [topic]
- Questions about [topic]
- Personal stories about [topic]

Each angle = a candidate newsletter topic.

### Step 2: Audience Archetype Integration

For each expanded angle, identify 3-5 relevant audience archetypes:
- Professional roles (freelancer, founder, employee, coach)
- Experience levels (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
- Demographic factors (industry, platform, geography)
- Situational contexts (building side business, escaping corporate, scaling existing)

Cross-reference: which angles + archetypes will resonate most with your primary reader right now?

### Step 3: Sub-Topic Variation

For each audience-specific angle, develop 2-3 related sub-topics that:
- Maintain relevance to the main topic
- Address natural progressions of interest (what comes next after this topic)
- Cover adjacent areas the reader also cares about
- Explore practical applications in the reader's specific context

## Publishing Frequency Rules

**Rule of thumb:** Shorter newsletter = more frequent. Longer newsletter = less frequent.

**The actual driver is "information speed":**

- **High-speed industry** (news, stocks, trending topics): Value is timeliness → shorter content, higher frequency
- **Low-speed industry** (writing craft, psychology, strategy, history): Value is depth → longer content, lower frequency

**Default starting point:** 1 newsletter per week. Adjust after 4-6 weeks of data.

**The 3 rules that keep you consistent:**
1. Publish everything you write — don't rewrite before you publish. You learn what to edit by putting it in front of readers.
2. Never skip a week — the moment you give yourself permission to skip, skipping becomes possible again.
3. Set a timer and publish what you have — 30-60 minutes, then send. This builds the muscle.

## Calendar Format

For each week of the 90-day calendar:

**Newsletter:**
- Headline (the draft title/subject line)
- Core topic category (from 10 Magical Ways)
- Target audience archetype
- Sub-topic focus
- Brief description (2-3 sentences — what the newsletter covers, what the reader walks away knowing)

**Supporting Social Content (7-8 posts per week):**
For each newsletter, generate 7-8 social post concepts:
- Vary content type: question, stat, tip, story, list, quote
- Include suggested format: text post, carousel, video concept, thread
- Each post should be thematically linked to the week's newsletter
- Match the audience archetype of the main newsletter

---

## Batching Strategy

Two productive approaches to actually writing all of this:

**Option A (Beginners):** Block 30-60 minutes per week. Write one newsletter. Publish it. Repeat.
Best for: building the habit, improving fast, learning what resonates.

**Option B (Scaling):** Block 3-4 hours every 2 weeks. Write 2-4 newsletters in one sitting.
Best for: writers who have built the skill and need to protect writing time from business demands.

Don't try Option B before you've done Option A for at least 8 weeks. You need enough data on what works before batching makes sense.

---

## Calendar Maintenance

Every 4 weeks, review:
- Which topics got the most opens?
- Which topics got the most replies?
- Which topics felt hardest to write?

Adjust the next 4 weeks of the calendar accordingly. The calendar is a starting point, not a contract.

High-engagement topics → write more in that frame and for that archetype.
Hard-to-write topics → either dig into why (misalignment with your expertise?) or swap for something else.
